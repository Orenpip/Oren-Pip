Published Research · NASA Ames Research Center · Summer 2024

NASA Search &
Rescue Drone

Designed a storm-condition aerial search-and-rescue vehicle integrating AI computer vision for autonomous victim detection — published in NASA's Technical Reports Server.

Published Research Drone Design AI Integration

Program

This project was completed through NASA's Aviation Concept Design Experience (ACDE) at Ames Research Center in summer 2024. The program places student researchers with NASA professionals to develop aviation concepts with real mission applicability.

The Design Problem

Search-and-rescue operations in severe weather are among the most dangerous and failure-prone aviation missions. Existing drone platforms are optimized for stable conditions — but the scenarios that most urgently require aerial support (storms, floods, hurricanes) are exactly the conditions where they fail.

The challenge was to design a drone platform that could operate reliably in high-wind, low-visibility storm conditions, and that could autonomously identify people in distress without requiring a trained remote operator for the detection task.

Wing Geometry Research

The first phase of the research focused on determining the optimal wing shape for storm-condition flight. I evaluated multiple airfoil profiles and planform geometries, modeling how each performed under varying wind speeds, gusts, and turbulent conditions. The final design prioritizes roll stability and low stall speed to enable slow, methodical search patterns even in high crosswinds.

AI Integration

Two AI systems were integrated into the drone's detection payload. An AI pose estimation model identifies human figures in camera frames — distinguishing people from debris and background clutter — and flags individuals who appear injured or immobile. A second AI voice-to-text model processes audio input to detect calls for help, extending detection range beyond visual line-of-sight.

Together, these systems enable the drone to operate with a high degree of autonomy, reducing operator cognitive load during high-stress rescue missions.

Publication

The research was presented to NASA professionals at the conclusion of the internship and co-authored as a technical memorandum submitted to the NASA Technical Reports Server.
